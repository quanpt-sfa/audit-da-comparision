\subsection{Applied empirical conclusions change selectively}\label{subsec:results_conclusion_sensitivity}

All 24 prespecified applied-consequence specifications are estimable after integrating auditor metadata. The common complete-case sample contains 7,320 issuer-years from 1,008 issuers. Models include pre-audit size, ROA, current ratio, short-term-debt intensity, loss status, fiscal-year fixed effects, and ICB Level-1 industry fixed effects, excluding the focal regressor from the remaining controls. Big Four status is available as an annual engagement attribute and is not imputed when missing.

Table~\ref{tab:research_conclusion_sensitivity} reports the modified-Jones results. Short-term-debt intensity is positively associated with signed DA in both states, but the post-audit coefficient is larger. Both the stacked interaction and paired-difference tests survive Benjamini--Hochberg adjustment. The corresponding high-$|DA|$ association remains strongly positive in both states but does not change significantly. Loss status is associated with more negative signed DA in both states, with a further negative post-audit difference under both tests. For high-$|DA|$, the loss coefficient changes from statistically insignificant to positive and significant, and both difference tests survive adjustment.

The Big Four result is less stable. Its signed-DA coefficient is statistically insignificant in the pre-audit state and weakly negative in the post-audit state. The stacked interaction is significant after adjustment, but the paired-difference estimate is not. Big Four status is unrelated to high-$|DA|$ in either state. Accordingly, the evidence does not support a robust Big Four conclusion from the paired design, despite a separate-state significance switch under Jones and modified Jones.

Across all four model families, 16 of 24 stacked interactions and 10 of 24 paired-difference coefficients have $q<0.05$, while four separate-state significance-status switches occur. The strongest cross-model pattern is the increase in the short-term-debt coefficient for signed DA: both difference tests reject under every model. The additional negative loss association with signed DA is also supported under every model. Loss--high-$|DA|$ state dependence is strongest under Jones and modified Jones; the interaction remains significant under the two performance-augmented models, but their paired-difference tests do not. Big Four signed-DA interactions are significant across models, yet the paired-difference tests are uniformly insignificant. These contrasts reinforce the reporting hierarchy in Section~\ref{subsec:conclusion_sensitivity_design}: separate-state significance changes and stacked interactions should not be interpreted as robust state dependence unless the paired-difference evidence agrees.

\subsection{Confirmatory decisions and execution limits}\label{subsec:results_confirmatory}

Table~\ref{tab:confirmatory_summary} applies the three prespecified intersection--union rules. The RQ1 family rejects because both Jones-family median component contrasts are positive. The RQ2 coverage family rejects because all three direct outside-gate shares exceed one half with issuer-cluster lower bounds above one half. The RQ2 excess-switching family rejects because CFO sign and all four model-specific DA-sign rates exceed their symmetric-sign benchmarks. All three conclusions remain after Holm adjustment.

The confirmatory results have three limits. First, the time-shift tests do not show that contemporaneous pairing produces uniquely larger component contrasts; the attribution result is therefore about observed component magnitude, not a causal effect of contemporaneous audit adjustment. Second, CFO-sign switching does not exceed the within-year signed-shift-reassignment benchmark, so its excess-switching conclusion is restricted to the symmetric-sign null. Third, the optional concentration and near-zero input files were absent from the completion bundle, leaving the supplemental-inference table empty. None of these limits overturns the three prespecified family decisions, but each narrows the claims that can be drawn from them.
